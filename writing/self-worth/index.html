Self-Worth: How Much Do You Value Yourself?

When worth has been tied to output, usefulness, and applause for too long, quiet moments can expose how fragile that foundation really is.

Munya Chipunza November 2025 3 min read

There are seasons when you can no longer tell the difference between your identity and your performance. You have been useful for so long that usefulness begins to feel like your deepest value.

You know how to produce, respond, carry, fix, provide, absorb, and keep things moving. People admire that. They lean on it. They thank you for it. Over time, their dependence can start to feel like proof that you matter.

But usefulness is not the same as worth. One can fluctuate daily. The other must survive the days when you are tired, uncertain, unseen, slower than usual, or unable to give what you normally give.

If your value only shows up when you are performing, it will disappear every time life forces you to be human.

Many of us were praised most when we were excelling, helping, coping, or being easy to depend on. So we learned to become dependable before we learned to become known. We learned to be impressive before we learned to be honest.

That creates a quiet fear: if I stop performing, will I still be loved? If I cannot provide what others expect, will I still have a place? These questions are rarely asked out loud, but they sit underneath burnout, people-pleasing, and the inability to rest without guilt.

The answer is not to become careless or passive. The answer is to remember that you are not a machine built to justify your own existence every morning. Your worth was not manufactured by productivity. It is older than your last success and more stable than your last failure.

Learning to believe that may take time. Sometimes it begins with small refusals: refusing to call yourself a failure on a slow day, refusing to confuse boundaries with selfishness, refusing to imagine that God only delights in the competent version of you.

Value yourself enough to remain a person, not just a function. That, too, is a holy discipline.
